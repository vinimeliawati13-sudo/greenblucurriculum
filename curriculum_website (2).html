Framework 01

The Greening Curriculum

A framework for teaching and learning that treats climate action not as a separate subject, but as a thread running through everything students study.

What is the Greening Curriculum?

The Greening Curriculum is an educational approach that brings sustainability into every classroom — not just science or geography. If students only ever hear about climate change in one subject, they will only ever think about it in one context. Real understanding requires seeing how it connects to history, economics, biology, and daily life.

This framework gives teachers the tools to make those connections visible. It shifts the focus from knowing facts about the environment to actually understanding how human systems interact with natural ones, and what students can do about it.

At its core, the Greening Curriculum develops students who are not just informed but capable of thinking critically and motivated to take part in shaping the world around them.

Aligned with UNESCO Education for Sustainable Development
Pillar 01

Systems Thinking

Students learn to see how climate, food, health, and economy are parts of the same interconnected system — understanding one means understanding all of them.

Pillar 02

Action-Oriented Learning

The goal is not just awareness. Students ask what they can actually do, whether changing habits, engaging with local government, or designing solutions to real community problems.

Pillar 03

Cross-Curricular Thinking

Sustainability shows up in Biology, Geography, Chemistry, Economics, and more. By connecting subjects, students build a fuller understanding than any single class can offer on its own.

Pillar 04

Community Connection

Learning does not stop at the classroom door. Students engage with local food systems, natural spaces, and communities as part of their education.

Pillar 05

Inquiry and Evidence

Students ask questions, look at data, and draw their own conclusions. This curriculum builds the skills to think well, not just to memorise what to think.

Pillar 06

Long-Term Values

The most important outcome is a genuine sense of responsibility. Students leave school caring about what happens to the world long after they graduate.

IGCSE Biology Integration

Ecosystems and Biodiversity through a Climate Lens

The Greening Curriculum gives students a reason to care about what they learn in Biology. When students study ecosystems, they are not just memorising food webs — they are looking at systems that are actively changing because of human decisions.

Food Webs
Food Webs and Energy Flow

Energy flows through an ecosystem from producers to consumers through feeding relationships. At each trophic level, around 90% of energy is lost as heat, which is why food chains rarely have more than four or five links.

Greening Connection
Disrupting one species in a food web can collapse the whole chain. The Greening Curriculum asks students to consider how agricultural land use and deforestation are already doing exactly that — and what protecting these systems looks like in practice.
Biodiversity
Biodiversity and Ecosystem Stability

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life in a given area, including genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity. Higher biodiversity generally makes an ecosystem more resilient and able to recover from disturbances like drought or disease.

Greening Connection
Biodiversity loss is one of the most urgent outcomes of climate change and habitat destruction. Students explore how protecting biodiversity is not just an environmental goal but a practical strategy for keeping food and water systems stable long term.
Carbon Cycle
Carbon and Nitrogen Cycles

Carbon moves between the atmosphere, living organisms, and the soil through photosynthesis, respiration, and decomposition. The nitrogen cycle similarly depends on bacteria to convert nitrogen into forms that plants and animals can use.

Greening Connection
Burning fossil fuels and clearing forests push extra carbon into the atmosphere, disrupting a cycle that took millions of years to balance. Students model what happens when these cycles are interrupted and what restoring forests and wetlands actually does.
Human Impact
Human Impact on Ecosystems

Human activities including agriculture, urbanisation, pollution, and overfishing have dramatically altered ecosystems around the world. The current rate of species extinction is estimated at 100 to 1000 times the natural background rate.

Greening Connection
This topic sits at the heart of the Greening Curriculum. Students examine case studies of ecosystems under pressure and are asked to evaluate what sustainable alternatives look like in practice — from rewilding to circular food systems.
Framework 02

The Blue Curriculum

A UNESCO toolkit for bringing Ocean Literacy into national education systems — helping students understand why the ocean matters and what their relationship with it actually means.

What is the Blue Curriculum?

Published by UNESCO's Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission in 2022, the Blue Curriculum is a practical guide for educators who want to bring ocean learning into formal schooling. It was developed as part of the UN Decade of Ocean Science for Sustainable Development, running from 2021 to 2030.

The central idea is Ocean Literacy — understanding the ocean's influence on us, and our influence on the ocean. With the ocean covering 70% of Earth's surface and providing food for over one billion people, this is foundational knowledge for anyone on this planet.

The Blue Curriculum does not propose a single fixed approach. It offers a Theory of Change model that can be adapted to fit different countries, cultures, and school systems — making it genuinely global in scope and application.

Part of the UN Decade of Ocean Science (2021 to 2030)
01

Knowledge and Awareness

Problem-based and wonder-based learning that makes ocean science feel real and relevant to students' lives.

02

Nature-Connectedness

Time spent learning in and around nature builds genuine emotional connection to the marine environment — something facts alone cannot produce.

03

Values and Attitudes

Developed through conversation with peers and teachers, guiding students toward choices that protect marine life and influence those around them.

04

Competences

The end goal is students who can actually do something — with the skills and confidence to take meaningful action in their communities.

IGCSE Biology Integration

Ecosystems and Biodiversity through an Ocean Lens

The Blue Curriculum brings ocean ecosystems to the centre of science education. When Biology students study biodiversity and ecological relationships, the ocean offers some of the most complex and fascinating examples on Earth — and some of the most threatened ones.

Marine Food Webs
Marine Food Webs

Ocean food webs begin with phytoplankton — microscopic algae that perform about half of all photosynthesis on Earth. These support zooplankton, small fish, larger predators, and eventually everything from dolphins to seabirds.

Blue Connection
Ocean warming and acidification are shifting the distribution of phytoplankton, which affects every organism above it in the food web. Students trace how a change at the base ripples outward and what that means for fishing communities worldwide.
Coral Reefs
Coral Reef Ecosystems

Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor but support around 25% of all marine species. They are built on the mutualistic relationship between coral polyps and photosynthetic algae called zooxanthellae.

Blue Connection
When ocean temperatures rise, corals expel their algae and bleach — losing both colour and the ability to survive. This is a direct, visible consequence of climate change that students can study using real monitoring data from reef observation networks worldwide.
Keystone Species
Keystone Species and Biodiversity

Some species have an outsized effect on their ecosystem. Sea otters control sea urchin populations, protecting kelp forests. Remove the otter, and the entire habitat can collapse — these are called keystone species because everything else depends on them.

Blue Connection
The Blue Curriculum uses examples like this to show that biodiversity is about understanding relationships, not just counting species. Students explore how overfishing removes keystones, and what marine protected areas can do to restore balance over time.
Nutrient Cycling
Nutrient Cycling in the Ocean

The biological pump is the process by which carbon is fixed by surface phytoplankton and sinks to the deep ocean when organisms die. It moves enormous amounts of carbon out of the atmosphere and stores it for centuries.

Blue Connection
If ocean warming disrupts phytoplankton, this pump weakens and more carbon stays in the atmosphere. Students connect the carbon cycle from Biology with the ocean's role as a carbon sink — and see why ocean health is inseparable from addressing climate change.

Both Curricula

Food Webs and Energy Flow

Energy flows from producers to consumers across trophic levels. At each step, about 90% is lost as heat — which is why food chains stay short and why one disruption can cascade through an entire ecosystem.

Greening CurriculumAgricultural expansion and deforestation remove producers from terrestrial food webs, triggering chain reactions that affect entire ecosystems and the communities that depend on them.
Blue CurriculumIn the ocean, phytoplankton loss from warming waters disrupts marine food webs from the base — affecting fish stocks, seabird populations, and coastal food security.
Both Curricula

Biodiversity and Ecosystem Stability

Biodiversity — genetic, species, and ecosystem variety — determines how resilient an ecosystem is. Higher biodiversity means more pathways for energy and nutrients, and more ability to recover from shocks.

Greening CurriculumOn land, habitat loss and monoculture farming are the leading drivers of biodiversity decline. Students evaluate how sustainable land use can protect species and ecosystem services simultaneously.
Blue CurriculumMarine biodiversity is equally threatened. Coral bleaching, ocean acidification, and overfishing are removing species faster than ecosystems can adapt — with consequences for billions of people.
Blue Curriculum Focus

Coral Reef Ecosystems

Covering less than 1% of the seafloor yet supporting 25% of all marine species, coral reefs are the ocean's most biodiverse ecosystems. Their survival depends on the mutualistic bond between coral polyps and zooxanthellae algae.

Blue CurriculumReef bleaching is one of the most visible signs of ocean warming. Students study live monitoring data, learn the biology behind bleaching events, and explore restoration efforts like coral gardening and heat-resistant coral breeding.
Both Curricula

Carbon and Nitrogen Cycles

Carbon and nitrogen cycle through the atmosphere, organisms, and soil via photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, and microbial activity. These cycles took millions of years to stabilise — and human activity is disrupting both within decades.

Greening CurriculumBurning fossil fuels and deforestation add carbon to the atmosphere while removing natural carbon stores. Students model tipping points and examine what large-scale reforestation and soil restoration actually achieve.
Blue CurriculumThe ocean's biological pump sequesters carbon for centuries. As warming weakens this pump, the ocean becomes less effective as a carbon sink — directly accelerating climate change.
Blue Curriculum Focus

Keystone Species

Keystone species have a disproportionately large effect on their ecosystem. Sea otters keep sea urchin populations in check — protecting kelp forests that shelter thousands of other species. Remove the otter and the habitat collapses.

Blue CurriculumStudents use keystone species as a lens to understand why biodiversity is relational, not just numerical. They examine how targeted conservation of a single species can stabilise an entire marine ecosystem and restore fisheries over time.
Both Curricula

Human Impact on Ecosystems

Agriculture, urbanisation, pollution, and overfishing have altered ecosystems faster than at any point in human history. The current species extinction rate is 100 to 1000 times the natural background rate.

Greening CurriculumStudents investigate how everyday decisions — what we eat, how we travel, what we buy — aggregate into global-scale ecosystem change, and what shifting those systems looks like from a policy and community level.
Blue CurriculumOcean pollution, plastic accumulation, and industrial fishing are examined as interconnected problems. Students evaluate marine protected areas, fishing quotas, and international agreements as real tools for ecosystem recovery.
